About Us

Our Mission

“To end the suffering of captive wild animals around the world, by uniting the world’s leading animal welfare organisations and captive wildlife facilities in providing expert, practical and sustainable solutions to improve animal welfare.”

Our Vision

“Is to create a world where every captive wild animal is able to thrive and live a good life.”

Our History

Founded in 2012, Wild Welfare is a UK registered charity working globally to improve welfare for wild animals living in captivity.

We are the first captive wild animal charity that is solely focused on improving welfare standards by uniting animal welfare NGOs and captive wildlife facilities, such as zoos, aquariums and sanctuaries, around the world.

'We forge links between global animal welfare NGOs, accredited zoo professionals and zoo associations, and work together on finding solutions to some of the most critical welfare issues facing wild animals in captivity.'

The Challenge

There are estimated to be more than 10,000 captive wild animal facilities worldwide, such as zoos, aquariums, sanctuaries and rescue centres. It is likely that only a very small percentage of these fall under appropriate animal welfare legislation and or guiding principles from an association. Poor captive animal welfare is often widely prevalent within facilities that fall outside recognised standards, meaning potentially thousands of animals are suffering around the world.

Globally, captive wild animal facilities significantly contribute to a diverse conservation effort, but sub-standard facilities, although often in the very countries struggling to manage regional declines in biodiversity, have little in the way of expertise and resources to contribute, limiting the value of the combined global effort. Zoos are in the spotlight for their animal care and welfare practices now more than ever, with societal interest in the welfare of all animals, including those in zoos, at an all-time high.

Some very poor zoos with extreme welfare concerns are regularly highlighted by a growing animal welfare community, with calls to boycott them or close them down. In many of these cases the reason for the outcry is obvious, but is closing them down really a long-term solution to the problem?
